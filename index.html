What is Origami?

Origami, derived from the Japanese words "ori" (folding) and "kami" (paper), is the traditional art of paper folding that transforms a flat sheet of paper into intricate three-dimensional forms without cutting or gluing. This centuries-old practice began in China and later flourished in Japan, becoming an essential cultural heritage. With its unique combination of simplicity and complexity, origami offers endless creative possibilities, from simple designs for beginners to elaborate sculptures for experts.

Akira Yoshizawa

Akira Yoshizawa (1911–2005) is widely regarded as the father of modern origami. Born in Japan, Yoshizawa transformed the traditional craft of paper folding into a sophisticated art form, introducing new techniques and elevating its status on a global scale. He developed the "wet-folding" technique, which uses dampened paper to create more sculptural, three-dimensional forms, allowing for greater expressiveness and intricate details in his creations.

Yoshizawa also made significant contributions to the global understanding of origami through his development of a symbolic notation system for folding instructions. Over his lifetime, Yoshizawa created over 50,000 models, although only a fraction were ever published in his books. His legacy continues to inspire origami enthusiasts and artists around the world.

Origin of ORIGAMI

Origami's history can be traced back to the 6th century when paper was a precious and rare commodity. During this time, folded paper was primarily used for ceremonial purposes, such as in religious rituals and traditional events. For example, folded paper designs like noshi were used as tokens of good luck or offerings. The early designs were simple, reflecting the limited availability of paper and the cultural significance attached to it. As time passed, paper became more affordable and accessible, allowing origami to evolve and spread beyond its ceremonial roots. By the Edo period (1603–1868), origami had become a recreational activity, with designs becoming increasingly elaborate and complex. This era also saw the introduction of paper folding techniques inspired by European influences, adding new dimensions to the craft. In the 20th century, origami reached new heights of creativity and recognition. Akira Yoshizawa, often regarded as the father of modern origami, introduced innovative techniques such as wet-folding, which allowed for more intricate and realistic designs. His contributions also included the development of the crease pattern system, which provided a universal language for sharing origami instructions worldwide. Yoshizawa’s work inspired a global interest in origami, leading to the formation of origami societies and its use in modern applications. Today, origami is celebrated as both an art and a science. Beyond its artistic appeal, it has practical applications in various fields, such as engineering, architecture, and medicine. For instance, origami principles are used in designing compact and deployable structures for space exploration, foldable solar panels, and even medical devices like stents. Its blend of creativity, mathematics, and precision continues to captivate and inspire people across the globe.
